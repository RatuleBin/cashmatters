The Case for Cash

Understanding why cash remains essential for society's resilience, privacy, and stability

Cash serves as a fundamental component of national critical infrastructure, functioning reliably during power outages, cyber attacks, natural disasters, and system-wide technological failures. Unlike digital systems that require continuous power, internet connectivity, and complex backend infrastructure, cash transactions require only two willing parties. This makes cash the ultimate backup system when other payment methods failâ€”ensuring economic continuity during crises and maintaining essential services when they're needed most.

The unique characteristics of cash payments guarantee privacy and personal integrity. Cash gives control to individuals over their personal payments data. Cash transactions don't collect any personal purchasing habits data, which could be used for marketing or surveillance, and are immune to digital fraud.

All forms of digital transactions pose inherent risks to privacy, including cybersecurity and hacking/data theft risksâ€”from cryptoassets and digital euro to bank transfers and card payments. Being able to pay by cash empowers each individual with the autonomy and agency to decide to what extent they want to expose themselves to cyber security risks, scams and fraud.

Cash plays a vital role in maintaining social bonds and cultural traditions. From gifting envelopes at weddings to charitable donations in places of worship, from children's pocket money teaching financial literacy to informal community support systems, cash facilitates social interactions that build community cohesion.

Digital alternatives often create barriers in these social contextsâ€”requiring bank accounts, smartphones, or technical knowledge that not everyone possesses. Cash remains the most universally understood and accessible medium for these important social exchanges that strengthen community bonds.

Read cultural studies

Cash is the most inclusive payment method ever created. It requires no bank account, no credit check, no internet connection, no smartphone, and no technical literacy. Approximately 1.4 billion adults worldwide remain unbanked, and billions more are underbanked or digitally excluded.

Vulnerable groups including the elderly, low-income individuals, rural populations, and those with disabilities rely on cash for daily transactions. Removing cash would effectively disenfranchise these populations from participating in the economy, exacerbating inequality and social exclusion.

Cash provides individuals with direct control over their financial resources without dependence on third-party intermediaries. In an era of increasing financial surveillance, account freezes, and algorithmic decision-making, cash represents the ultimate form of financial sovereignty.

This autonomy is crucial for:

  • Emergency preparedness: Physical savings accessible during banking crises
  • Financial planning: Tangible budgeting without digital tracking
  • Personal security: Protection against identity theft and digital fraud
  • Economic independence: Freedom from corporate and government surveillance
Learn about financial sovereignty

Cash serves as a reliable store of value backed by sovereign guarantee. Unlike digital currencies that can fluctuate wildly or lose value due to technological failures, and unlike bank deposits that represent claims rather than actual ownership, physical cash represents immediate, unconditional value.

This characteristic makes cash essential for:

  • Emergency savings that are immediately accessible
  • Protection against inflation when held as physical currency
  • Financial stability during banking crises
  • Tangible wealth that cannot be "turned off" by third parties

Physical cash serves as the foundational anchor for monetary systems, providing a tangible reference point for value that people can understand and trust. This trust is essential for financial stability and public confidence in the monetary system.

Cash provides:

  • A psychological anchor for understanding money's value
  • A check on potential negative interest rates
  • A mechanism for maintaining public trust in the monetary system
  • Stability during periods of digital currency volatility
  • A safeguard against excessive financialization of the economy
